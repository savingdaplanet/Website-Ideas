NASA’s X-59 Quiet SuperSonic Technology X-plane

NASA’s X-59 Quiet SuperSonic Technology X-plane, or QueSST, has successfully completed its inaugural flight, showcasing its ability to fly faster than the speed of sound without producing a loud, disruptive sonic boom. Instead of the traditional sonic boom, communities beneath the flight path experience a relatively quiet sonic thump – if they notice it at all. This groundbreaking technology will pave the way for potential regulations that could permit commercial supersonic flight over land.

The X-59 has been actively flying over select communities as part of its mission to gather real-time data on public reactions to sonic thump noise. This data is crucial for informing U.S. and international regulators as they consider new guidelines surrounding sound levels for supersonic flight.

Following years of dedicated research and development, the X-59 is a symbol of NASA's pioneering efforts to revolutionize aviation, promising significantly reduced flight times for passengers traveling internationally.

Assembly of the X-59 took place at Lockheed Martin’s Skunk Works facility in Palmdale, California, where engineers worked diligently to bring the aircraft to life. Significant advancements were made in constructing its eXternal Vision System (XVS), an innovative solution that provides pilots with a forward-looking view through a high-tech display system, compensating for the absence of a traditional cockpit window.

The X-59’s major components, including the F414-GE-100 turbofan engine, the aircraft’s vertical tail, and its iconic, elongated nose, have been successfully installed, marking key milestones in the timeline of the project.
